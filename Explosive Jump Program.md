### Day 1: Maximum Force & Contrast Training

Focus: Absolute strength paired with explosive movements (Complex Training) to trick the nervous system into firing harder.

1. Dynamic Warm-up (10-15 mins)

- Pogo jumps (focus on stiff ankles): 3 sets of 15 seconds
    
- Walking lunges, leg swings, and hip openers
    

2. The Contrast Pairing (Post-Activation Potentiation) Perform A1, rest 90 seconds, perform A2, rest 3 minutes. Repeat for 4 total rounds.

- A1. Barbell Back Squat: 4 sets of 3–5 reps (Heavy, 80-85% of your 1 Rep Max). Focus on moving the bar fast on the way up.
    
- A2. Max Effort Box Jumps: 4 sets of 3 reps. (Jump as high as possible; the box just reduces the impact on landing).
    

3. Loaded Power

- B. Trap Bar Jumps or Dumbbell Jump Squats: 4 sets of 4 reps. (Use a light weight, roughly 20-30% of your body weight. Explode off the floor). Rest 2 minutes between sets.
    

4. Unilateral & Tendon Health

- C. Bulgarian Split Squats: 3 sets of 6-8 reps per leg. (Moderate weight, controlled eccentric/lowering phase).
    
- D. Seated Calf Raises: 3 sets of 12 reps. (Heavy, with a 2-second pause at the bottom to build Achilles tendon strength).

### Day 2: Velocity & Fast Stretch-Shortening Cycle (SSC)

Focus: Pure speed, minimizing ground contact time, and moving moderate weights as fast as possible.

1. Dynamic Warm-up (10-15 mins)

- A-skips, B-skips, broad jumps (sub-maximal).
    

2. Fast Plyometrics

- A. Depth Jumps: 4 sets of 4 reps. (Step off a 12 to 18-inch box. The instant your feet hit the floor, rebound vertically as fast and high as possible. Cue: Pretend the floor is lava.) Rest 2-3 minutes between sets.
    

3. Olympic Lifting / Ballistic Movement

- B. Hang Power Cleans OR Kettlebell Swings: 4 sets of 3–5 reps. (If you don't know Olympic lifting techniques, do heavy, explosive kettlebell swings). Rest 2 minutes between sets.
    

4. Posterior Chain Power

- C. Romanian Deadlifts (RDLs): 3 sets of 6–8 reps. (Focus on a deep hamstring stretch, then aggressively snap the hips forward).
    
- D. Nordic Hamstring Curls: 3 sets of 4-6 reps. (Crucial for decelerating forces and preventing hamstring pulls when sprinting/jumping).

### 1. Calculating Weight for Strength (Bulgarian Split Squats, RDLs)

For absolute strength, we use the Reps in Reserve (RIR) scale. This is a science-backed way to autoregulate your training based on how hard the set feels.

For your Bulgarian Split Squats and Single-Leg RDLs, the program calls for sets of 5 to 8 reps. You want an RIR of 1 or 2. This means by the time you hit your final rep, you physically could only do 1 or 2 more reps with good form if your life depended on it.

How to find your backpack weight:

1. Load the bag: Put what feels like a moderately heavy amount of weight into the backpack (books, rice bags, dumbells, etc.).
    
2. Do a test set: Perform a set of Bulgarian Split Squats on your weaker leg.
    
3. Evaluate: Stop at 5 reps. Ask yourself: "How many more could I have done?"
    

- If the answer is "I could have done 5 more" $\rightarrow$ The bag is too light. Add weight.
    
- If the answer is "I couldn't have done a single extra rep" $\rightarrow$ The bag is slightly too heavy. Take a little weight out.
    
- If the answer is "I probably had 1 or 2 reps left in the tank" $\rightarrow$ You found your perfect working weight.
    

  

### 2. Calculating Weight for Loaded Jumps (Backpack Jump Squats)

For power movements, the goal is velocity. If the weight is so heavy that you jump slowly, you are defeating the purpose of the exercise. Science shows that peak power output during jump squats happens at roughly 20% to 30% of your body weight.

The Math:

- If you weigh 150 lbs, you want roughly 30 to 45 lbs in your bag.
    
- If you weigh 180 lbs, you want roughly 35 to 55 lbs in your bag.
    

How to test it: Put the backpack on, secure it tightly, and jump. You should still feel explosive. If you feel like you are struggling to get off the ground, remove some books or water jugs. How to protect your tendons from getting worse over time

To make sure your joints don't degrade from repeated use, you need to implement these three non-negotiable rules into your training life:

- The 3-to-1 Deload Rule: You cannot go 100% every single week. For every 3 weeks of hard training, the 4th week must be a "deload" week. During this week, you cut the weight in your backpack by 20% and cut your total sets in half. This is when your tendons actually heal and your vertical jump catches up to your hard work.
    
- Feed the Tendon with Heavy/Slow Movements: The heavy backpack split squats and RDLs in the program are your armor. Do not rush the lowering (eccentric) phase of these lifts. Taking 3 to 4 seconds to lower yourself down under heavy weight is exactly what thickens the tendon and prevents Jumper's Knee.
    
- Auto-Regulation (Listen to the "Creak"): If you wake up on Thursday for Day 2 (Fast SSC) and your knees feel achy, stiff, or "creaky," do not do the Drop Jumps or Tuck Jumps. Swap them out for heavy, slow exercises or isometric holds (like a heavy wall sit). Tendons do not care about your ego; if you push through joint pain to do plyometrics, you will set yourself back months.
    

###  How to Deload Your Legs (The Backpack Program) 

This is where the real recovery happens. We are going to protect your tendons and recharge your CNS.

- The Strength Lifts: Reduce the weight in your backpack by 20% to 30%. Cut your sets down to 2 sets per exercise. Move the weight slowly and focus entirely on perfect form.
    
- The Plyometrics (Crucial): Remove the highest-impact jumps entirely for this week.
    

- Skip the Drop Jumps and Max Effort Tuck Jumps.
    
- Replace them with low-impact, bouncy movements like standard Pogo Jumps (2 sets of 15 seconds) just to keep the ankles springy without the harsh landings.
    

Since you are rolling, you won't have a perfect "Week 4” deload. Instead, just count your Leg days. Every time you hit your 7th and 8th Leg workouts in the cycle, that is your Deload phase. Drop the backpack weight by 20%, cut all sets to 2, and skip the heavy jumps for those two sessions.

### The 4 Rules for a Rolling Schedule

1. The "Green Light" Days (Legs land on Mon, Fri, Sat, or Sun)

- What to do: Do absolutely nothing to the schedule. Run your heavy backpack program exactly as written.
    
- Why: You have at least 48 hours of buffer before or after your Wednesday game. Your central nervous system (CNS) will be perfectly fine.
    

2. The Tuesday Trap (Legs land on Tuesday)

- The Problem: If you do heavy plyometrics and squats on Tuesday, your legs will feel like concrete for your game on Wednesday.
    
- The Fix (Swap): Take your Rest day on Tuesday instead. On Wednesday, treat your basketball game as your Leg day. Do 15 minutes of slow tendon work (Nordics, Backpack Calf Raises) either before you head to the court or when you get home. Resume Push on Thursday.
    
- If the Maximum Force day lands here, treat the next leg day as Velocity
    

3. The Direct Hit (Legs land on Wednesday)

- The Problem: A direct schedule collision.
    
- The Fix (Merge): Do not do the backpack jumps. Play your game (which covers your explosive plyometrics), and do your 15 minutes of heavy/slow tendon armor work. Take your Rest day on Thursday.
    

4. The Post-Game Hangover (Legs land on Thursday)

- The Problem: A competitive game on Wednesday night completely drains your CNS. If you try to do heavy backpack jumps on Thursday morning, your force output will be terrible.
    
- The Fix (Push): Swap your Rest day and your Leg day. Rest on Thursday to let your CNS reboot, and crush your Leg day on Friday.
